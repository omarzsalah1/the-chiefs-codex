# THE CHIEFS CODEX - Chapter 3
## "On Radical Candor: Care and Challenge"

Power resides not in the prince's sword, but in the gatekeeper's ear. Courts swarm with suitors bearing gifts and grievances. Each seeks audience, favor, or revenge. The prince hears all, grants all, and falls. The gatekeeper filters the flood. He admits the vital, bars the venomous. Without him, the throne drowns in petitions. Human nature compels men to approach the mighty. Nobles plot alliances through whispers. Merchants offer loans laced with hooks. Factions forge letters of false loyalty. The gatekeeper stands as the first wall. He discerns truth from flattery. Care must burn in his chest for the prince's survival. Yet challenge must sharpen his tongue. Radical candor alone preserves the realm. He who pities the petitioner invites ruin. He who flatters the prince courts death. Therefore, the gatekeeper must know how to act as the beast: he must be fox enough to recognize the traps concealed in honeyed words, and lion enough to frighten the wolves who circle the throne in courtier's clothing. The court of Valdaro teaches this eternal law: gates unguarded crumble first.

In the March of Valdaro, Prince Ercole ruled from the stone fortress of Castelnuovo. His court drew envoys from Milan, Florence, and the Papal envoys from Rome. Bertoldo served as gatekeeper. Tall, scarred from border skirmishes, he wore a plain doublet of black wool. No jewels adorned him. His eyes missed nothing. Bertoldo cared for Ercole as a brother. They had ridden together against raiders from Ferrara. Yet Bertoldo challenged Ercole without mercy. He turned away half the visitors. "The prince has no time for merchants of wind," he would say.

One dawn, a rider arrived from Florence. Giacomo di Rossi, a noble of middling house, dismounted in the courtyard. Dust cloaked his velvet cloak. He carried a sealed letter from Lorenzo de' Medici's agent. Giacomo demanded instant audience. "The matter concerns the prince's life," he declared. Guards led him to Bertoldo in the antechamber. Torchlight flickered on marble floors. Bertoldo sat behind an oak table strewn with parchments. He broke the seal without haste. The letter promised alliance. Florence offered gold for Valdaro's condottieri in a war against Milan. In return, Ercole must seize Ferrara's border lands. Giacomo leaned forward. "Prince Lorenzo values Valdaro's strength. This pact secures your borders forever."

Bertoldo read in silence. He cared for Ercole's realm. Valdaro's fields fed its people. War would burn them. Milan loomed north, its duke Francesco Sforza ever watchful. Florence played both sides, as always. Consequently, Bertoldo folded the letter. "The prince hears no such offers today." Giacomo flushed. "You dare refuse Medici gold?" Bertoldo met his eyes. "Gold that chains. Return to Florence. Tell Lorenzo Valdaro bows to no banker-prince." Giacomo spat. "Ercole will regret this insult." Bertoldo signaled guards. They dragged Giacomo out. The noble swore vengeance as iron gates clanged shut.

Word spread. Giacomo reached Ferrara by dusk. There, he met Count Alesso, a rival to Ercole. Alesso hosted factions plotting against Valdaro. Giacomo relayed the slight. "Bertoldo bars the door. Ercole hears nothing of worth." Alesso nodded. He dispatched his own man, Vito, a sly scribe with a forged missive. Vito arrived at midday, posing as a papal courier. His ring bore the fisherman's seal. "Holy Father Nicholas sends word," Vito said. Bertoldo examined the ring. Wax was fresh, but the script trembled. "Papal couriers ride in pairs," Bertoldo said. Vito stammered. "This haste allowed no second." Bertoldo cared for Ercole's soul and state. Popes meddled, as Sixtus would later prove. He challenged directly. "You bear lies from Ferrara. Speak truth or face the cells." Vito confessed. Alesso plotted to poison Ercole's wine at the next banquet. Hence, Bertoldo seized him. Guards extracted names. Three kitchen hands served Alesso. Bertoldo hanged them at dawn. Vito rotted in the dungeon.

Ercole heard the full tale that eve. In the solar, fire crackled. Bertoldo laid out the letters. "Florence seeks your spears for their Milan feud. Ferrara sends poison. Both test your gate." Ercole paced. "Why bar Giacomo? Medici gold could arm us." Bertoldo stood firm. He cared enough to wound. "Gold from Florence drags you into their wars. Sforza crushes us next. Alesso strikes while you feast. I turned the Florentine away. I broke the Ferrarese. You live because I filter." Ercole struck the table. "You presume too much." Bertoldo did not flinch. "Presume? I preserve. Care without challenge kills princes. Admit all, and courtiers devour you." Ercole silenced. Dawn brought envoys from Venice. Bertoldo admitted one: a doge's factor with trade terms, no strings. Therefore, Ercole signed. Valdaro's granaries filled.

Months passed. Spring rains swelled the Arno-like river bordering Valdaro. Scouts reported Milanese lancers massing. Sforza aimed south. Florence wavered, their alliance hollow. Ercole summoned ministers. "We stand alone." Bertoldo spoke. "Not alone. Venice trades. Our borders hold." A spy arrived, cloaked. He whispered of Giacomo's return. The noble now rode with Alesso's banner. They camped in hills, hiring condottieri. Giacomo penned a new plea: "Join us against Milan, or perish." Bertoldo burned it unread. He cared for Ercole's blood. That night, assassins scaled the walls. Bertoldo waited in shadow. His blade found two throats. Dawn revealed Giacomo at the gate, demanding parley. "Traitor-gatekeeper! Face me!"

Bertoldo descended. Courtyard rang with steel. Giacomo lunged, rapier flashing. Bertoldo parried. "You bring death for a barred door." Giacomo sneered. "Ercole rots without friends." Bertoldo pressed. His dagger nicked Giacomo's arm. Blood soaked velvet. "Friends like poison? Like Florentine chains?" Giacomo faltered. Bertoldo's thrust pierced his chest. The noble fell, gasping. "Alesso... comes..." Bertoldo knelt. "Tell him the gate holds." Giacomo died. Ercole watched from battlements. Cheers rose from Valdaro's men.

Alesso's host struck at midsummer. Five hundred horse, banners snapping. Valdaro's walls repelled them. Bertoldo led sorties. He captured Alesso's herald. Under torch, the man broke. Alesso fled to Ferrara, shamed. Milan halted, wary of Valdaro's resolve. Florence sent no gold, their plot exposed. Ercole raised Bertoldo in council. "Your candor saved us." Bertoldo bowed. "Care and challenge. Nothing more." Valdaro endured. The gate stood firm.

The lesson extracts thus: the gatekeeper embodies radical candor. He cares personally for the prince's life and realm. This care demands sacrificeâ€”he turns away gold, nobles, kin if needed. Yet he challenges directly. No soft words veil truth. "This offer kills you," he declares. Not "Perhaps decline." Consequences follow the want of it. Admit flatterers, and factions fracture the court. Bar all challengers, and blindness breeds defeat. Bertoldo acted. He read letters with prince's eyes. He weighed motives against Valdaro's needs. Giacomo's gold tempted war. Vito's poison promised quick end. Bertoldo rejected both. He confronted Ercole: "Filter or fall." The reader learns the act. Stand at the gate. Know the prince's perils. Care enough to deny. Challenge enough to instruct. Test every petitioner: Does he serve the realm or self? Speak truth like steel. He who masters this wields power unseen. Princes rise on such shoulders. Courts crumble without.

_*Gates yield to neither pity nor flattery. The gatekeeper's candor is the throne's silent sword.*_
